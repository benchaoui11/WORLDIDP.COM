Driving in Iceland: What to Confirm Before You Travel

We have not completed source-backed research for Iceland, so this page does not state whether an International Driving Permit is required. Iceland drives on the right. Iceland is party to the 1949 Geneva Convention. Confirm current requirements with the relevant national authority and your rental provider before you drive.

Which convention applies

Iceland is party to the 1949 Geneva Convention. The convention determines which IDP format has a legal basis here, which is why two IDPs that look similar are not always interchangeable.

Is a digital IDP accepted in Iceland?

Not confirmed. No published Icelandic national policy on digital-only driving documents was found. The practical requirement for visitors is the original licence itself.

This is the least documented question in this category. Most national authorities have not published a position on digital-only driving documents, and rental companies set their own rules. Carry a printed document and treat any digital copy as a convenience, not a substitute. See our explanation of what an IDP is for more.

What to carry

  • Original valid national driving licence
  • Translation or IDP if your licence is not in the Latin alphabet
  • Passport
  • Rental agreement and F-road insurance where applicable

Your original national driving licence is the document that grants driving privileges. A translation-support document is presented alongside it and never replaces it.

Rental desks and roadside checks in Iceland

At the rental desk. The rental questions that matter in Iceland are rarely about the licence. Sand and ash protection, gravel protection and river-crossing exclusions are what determine whether you are covered. F-roads — the interior highland routes — legally require a 4x4 and are excluded from most standard rental policies entirely.

In a roadside check. Iceland has no motorway network in the continental sense; the Ring Road is largely single carriageway at 90 km/h. Speed limits drop to 80 km/h on gravel, and single-lane bridges and blind summits are common. Off-road driving outside marked tracks is a criminal offence with substantial fines.

Specific to Iceland

  • Iceland is party to the 1949 Geneva Convention.
  • There are no motorways as such — 90 km/h on paved rural roads, 80 km/h on gravel.
  • F-roads in the highlands legally require a four-wheel-drive vehicle and are seasonally closed.
  • Driving off marked roads or tracks is a criminal offence, not a rental-policy matter.
  • Standard rental insurance typically excludes sand and ash damage, gravel damage and river crossings.

Why this page does not state a requirement

We publish a requirement only when we can point to an official source and record the date we checked it. That research is not yet complete for Iceland, so this page deliberately does not tell you whether an IDP is required.

For transparency: an internal dataset records this destination as "recommended", which we have not yet confirmed against an official source; and a motorway limit of 90 km/h is recorded but unconfirmed.

Confirm with the national road authority or embassy for Iceland, and separately with your rental provider, whose contractual rules can be stricter than the law.

Iceland driving document questions

Do you need an International Driving Permit in Iceland?

Generally not, if your licence is full, valid and written in the Latin alphabet. A licence in a different script is where a translation or IDP becomes useful.

Can I drive an F-road in Iceland with a normal rental car?

No. F-roads legally require a four-wheel-drive vehicle, are closed outside the summer season, and are excluded from most standard rental insurance policies. River crossings are excluded even with a 4x4 on many policies.

What are the speed limits in Iceland?

50 km/h in built-up areas, 80 km/h on gravel roads and 90 km/h on paved rural roads. Iceland has no motorway network in the continental European sense.

Is it illegal to drive off-road in Iceland?

Yes. Driving outside marked roads and tracks is a criminal offence carrying substantial fines, not simply a breach of your rental agreement.
